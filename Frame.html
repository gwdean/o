1.2 Contents of Frame Analysis

  • Introduction
    Elementary Terms (page 10)

    Strip Any arbitrary slice or cut from the stream of ongoing activity…as seen from the perspective of those subjectively involved in sustaining an interest in them.

    Frame The term derives from Bateson.

    The definitions of a situation are built up in accordance with principles of organization which govern events– at least social ones-- and our subjective involvement in them; frame is the word I use to refer to such of these basic elements as I am able to identify.

    Frame Analysis A slogan to refer to the examination in these terms of the organization of experience.

    Organization of Experience (13) This book is about the organization of experience-- something that an individual actor can take into his mind–and not the organization of society.

    No claims are made about the core matters of sociology: social organization and social structure.

    The study addresses the structure of experience individuals have at any moment of their social lives.

    After a 16 page introduction, the chapter "Introduction" then switches in structure and tone. The next four pages consist of nine sections, each of which comments on the others.

    That is what frame analysis is all about.

  • Primary Frameworks
    A primary framework is one that is seen as rendering what would otherwise be a meaningless aspect of the scene into something that is meaningful.

    Each primary framework allows its user to locate, perceive, identify, and label a seemingly infinite number of concrete occurrences defined in its terms.

    Two broad classes of primary frameworks: natural and social

    Natural Frameworks Identify occurrences seen as undirected, unoriented, unanimated, unguided, "purely physical".

    Social frameworks Provide background understanding for events that incorporate the will, aim, and controlling effort of an intelligence, a live agency, the chief one being the human being.
